**Ep. 248 — Feature Flags & A/B Testing: A Deep Dive with Ishan Khanna**
*Fragmented · Don Felker & Kaushik Gopal · 66 min · 2023-06-26*

Ishan Khanna, Android GDE and engineer at Tinder (formerly Booking.com), joins to break down feature flagging and A/B testing from first principles, grounded in hard-won production experience.

The episode opens with a formative story from Booking.com: Ishan proposed migrating from Picasso to Glide, confident the switch would improve performance metrics. Instead of just shipping it, he designed an A/B test using a strategy pattern — an `AsyncImageView` that accepted pluggable image-loading strategies, letting him flip between Picasso and Glide per user cohort. After running the experiment for the required 2–4 weeks, results were flat. No measurable business impact. The lesson: technical improvements must be validated against business outcomes, not assumed. Booking.com's culture of "commercial awareness" — expecting every engineer to quantify the value of their changes — was the forcing function. A useful industrial benchmark surfaced here: only 1–2 out of every 10 experiments succeed, which is why experiments must be scoped to the smallest possible change that can still produce a detectable signal.

On the conceptual distinction: A/B testing is the methodology (collect data, compare outcomes); feature flagging is the technical mechanism that makes it possible. You can have feature flags without running experiments (e.g., for staged rollouts or kill switches), but you can't A/B test in a mobile app without some form of dynamic configuration — which is itself a feature flag by another name. Control group = current behavior, variant group = the change under test. Multiple variants are valid (Google famously tested nine shades of blue). On statistical power: small sample sizes produce high false-positive rates. The p-value captures confidence in the result, and that confidence requires sufficient sample size — meaning early-stage apps can technically run experiments but should understand their results carry low statistical weight.

**Why it's worth your time:** If you've ever pushed a library migration or UI refactor on instinct and called it done, this episode reframes what "done" means. The Picasso/Glide story alone is worth 10 minutes, and the feature flag vs. A/B testing distinction clears up terminology confusion that trips up most teams.
